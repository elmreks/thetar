Technical Achievement Registry

TAR is the world’s leading authority on the certification of technically valid accomplishments: achievements that may be trivial, dubious, reckless, bureaucratically suspicious, or otherwise deeply unnecessary, but which technically occurred.

This project treats absurd human behavior with the dry procedural seriousness of a standards body, legal archive, and nonprofit bureaucracy that should not exist but unfortunately does.

Operating Principles

The joke works best when the institution stays serious, the language stays formal, and the underlying achievement is ridiculous enough to deserve documentation.

1. Technical Validity

An event qualifies when it technically occurred, regardless of dignity, usefulness, athletic merit, or whether anyone involved should have gone home earlier.

2. Deadpan Bureaucracy

All copy should read like a standards agency, legal filing, or nonprofit certification board documenting nonsense with inappropriate confidence.

3. Witness Flexibility

Acceptable evidence includes photos, screenshots, bar staff confirmation, witness statements, and stories that are simply too specific to be fabricated well.

Regulatory Framework

The institution should publish rules as though they were drafted by a committee of dry attorneys, mildly hungover administrators, and one very committed copywriter.

Selected Articles

  1. Article 1 — Technical Validity. An achievement shall be recognized when it technically occurred.
  2. Article 2 — Witness Standards. Valid witnesses may include bar staff, friends, strangers, and anybody willing to say “yeah, I saw that.”
  3. Article 3 — Evidence Requirements. Evidence may consist of photos, video, screenshots, or compelling narrative detail.
  4. Article 4 — Jurisdiction. TAR recognizes accomplishments occurring globally, locally, in bars, in basements, on sidewalks, and on skateboards.
  5. Article 5 — Appeals Process. Appeals shall be reviewed and ignored in the order received.

Commercial Potential

The concept is intentionally stupid in a way that can become useful: bar record nights, sponsor-backed challenge events, cheap paid certifications, community submissions, and bureaucratic fake-merch that people actually want to wear.

Potential lines include “Certified by TAR,” “Technically a Champion,” and “Officially Dubious Achievement Holder.”
